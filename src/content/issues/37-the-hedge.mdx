There are places that are neither destinations nor wilderness.

You pass them every day.

The strip of shrubs behind the grocery store. The overgrown lot between two houses. The line where the mowed grass stops and the sumac begins.

For me, one of them is the hedge behind my building in Algoma.

Not a storybook hedge. Not hawthorn. Not neat enough to photograph.

Just a long tangle of lilac, volunteer maple, dogwood, grapevine, and whatever else has quietly negotiated a truce there over the last fifty years. It runs along the back edge of the alley behind the old brick storefront where I live and work. Delivery trucks idle nearby. Snowbanks collect there in winter.

In June, before the leaves fully settle into themselves, the Song Sparrows start before the street does.

There is a gap in the middle where people sometimes cut through.

Not enough to become a path.

Just enough to suggest one.

Not forest.

Not field.

Not cultivated.

Not forgotten.

Just enough outside.

When I started writing this, I thought that hedge was the beginning of the idea.

Then I remembered another one.

Years ago, I worked at an ad agency in Minneapolis in one of those places that seems assembled more than designed—industrial park, office strip, loading dock, retaining wall, corporate shrubs.

Out front was a retention pond.

One spring a Canada goose nested there.

During my breaks I would sit outside and watch her. She seemed impossibly committed to a place nobody would intentionally visit. Cars moved behind her. Someone always seemed to be smoking nearby. Wind pushed wrappers across the parking lot.

She sat.

Then one day the goslings appeared.

Over the next weeks I watched them become themselves.

Out back there was a retaining wall and below it a packed dirt strip under trees and an unmanaged hedge where trash accumulated—paper cups, blown bags, fragments of office life.

But if you were a child, it would have been perfect.

A fort.

A hideout.

A kingdom.

Cool dirt. Low branches. Hidden entrances.

You could imagine spending an entire summer there.

I did not have language for it then.

I have language for it now.

Even there.

Especially there.

I was looking for the hedge.

For a long time I thought of places like these as incomplete landscapes—places waiting to become something. Cleared. Built. Improved.

Now I wonder if they already are something.

A hedge.

Not the clipped green wall of estate gardens.

An older hedge.

A living edge.

Historically, a hedge was practical. It marked boundaries. Kept animals in. Kept animals out. Separated one person’s responsibility from another’s.

But older language occasionally preserved stranger meanings.

One possible root offered for the old English word hægtesse—sometimes translated loosely as hedge-rider, hedge-dweller, or the figure later collapsed into our modern image of the witch—places her not at the center of the village but along its edge.

Whether the etymology survives perfectly matters less to me than the image.

Not keeper of secret knowledge.

Keeper of crossings.

Because a hedge was rarely only one thing.

It was shelter.

A corridor.

A place for berries and birds.

A place where tools disappeared.

A place children entered and emerged changed.

A place where seeds accumulated.

A place where people paused.

You could stand at the hedge and still belong to both worlds.

The hedge is not wilderness enough for awe.

Not productive enough for admiration.

Not tidy enough for approval.

And yet I suspect most of life actually happens there.

Friendships rarely begin at milestones and ceremonies.

They begin leaning on fences.

Ideas rarely arrive fully formed at desks.

They appear while washing dishes or driving home.

Children become themselves in margins—in forts, alleys, under tables, behind garages.

Even grief does not live entirely in loss or recovery.

It lives in the hedge for a long while.

So does joy.

The hedge is where one thing becomes another.

Summer becomes autumn.

Path becomes woods.

Acquaintance becomes friend.

Interest becomes craft.

House becomes home.

Morning becomes day.

None of these transitions happen all at once.

We cross them gradually.

A few years ago, someone removed an old hedgerow outside town to widen parking and improve visibility.

It made complete sense.

Cleaner lines.

Easier maintenance.

Afterward I noticed the absence before I understood it.

The birds moved.

Snow drifted differently.

The road looked wider but felt smaller.

There was less uncertainty.

And less life.

Because the hedge is where strange combinations survive.

The hedge allows two truths to remain neighbors.

You can be skeptical and enchanted.

Practical and reverent.

Ordinary and alive.

Rooted and changing.

You do not have to choose a camp and stay there forever.

You can stand at the edge awhile.

You can listen.

You can let one world speak to another.

Perhaps that is why hedges appear so often in stories.

Cross the hedge.

Meet at the hedge.

Do not cross after dark.

Follow the hedge line.

Maybe the warning was never that danger waited there.

Maybe it was simply this:

Pay attention.

You are entering a place where categories loosen.

This year, that is what these essays are for.

Not escape.

Not certainty.

Just repeated visits.

Forty-eight times over the turning of a year, I want to walk the edges of ordinary things and see what gathers there.

Weather.

Objects.

Places.

Conversations.

Work.

Memory.

The overgrown lot.

The kitchen window.

The ditch.

The shoreline.

The alley behind the brick building.

I want to spend more time at the hedge.

Not because the ordinary world is less real.

Quite the opposite.

I want to become more available to it.

To notice where one season becomes another.

To leave small patches unmapped.

To speak to neighbors a little longer.

To walk without arriving.

To let questions remain questions.

To remember that boundaries can be habitats.

And if these essays do anything at all, I hope they make the edges of your own life easier to recognize once you leave them.
